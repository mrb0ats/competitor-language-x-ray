# Competitor Language X-Ray

Research ecommerce markets the way a performance marketer would: competitors, offers, funnels, product claims, ad angles, reviews, and the exact language buyers use before they purchase.

The skill has three jobs:

1. **Ecommerce competitor read**: brand/product positioning, funnel, offers, price architecture, proof, reviews, Shopify app/theme stack, paid-social strategy, and threat ranking.
2. **Buyer-language read**: raw customer words, desires, objections, failed attempts, tone, awareness level, and proof demands.
3. **Execution synthesis**: core avatar, sub-avatars, validated mass desires, angle map, offer gaps, landing-page gaps, and next tests.

Use live sources. Do not fabricate. Treat "not found" as a valid finding.

## Scope

Prioritize ecommerce and paid-social use cases:

- DTC brands, Shopify stores, Shopify app and theme detection, product pages, landing pages, advertorials, funnels, Amazon listings, TikTok Shop, Etsy, Meta shops, supplement/beauty/pet/home/gadget/fashion/wellness products, dropshipping products, and creator-led physical-product brands.
- Competitor ads, Meta Ad Library, TikTok Creative Center, Google Ads Transparency Center, Shopify app detectors, Shopify theme detectors, UGC, native image ads, VSLs, advertorials, PDPs, bundles, discounts, guarantees, subscriptions, upsells, OCU/post-purchase flows, email/SMS offers, and CRO.
- Customer language from Reddit, niche forums, YouTube/TikTok comments, Amazon reviews, Trustpilot, Google reviews, app/marketplace reviews, competitor one-star reviews, search autocomplete, People Also Ask, and public communities.

Avoid drifting into generic software, service, info-product, agency, or audience analysis unless the user explicitly asks and it supports an ecommerce decision.

## Choose The Mode

Use the smallest mode that satisfies the user:

- **Ecom Competitor X-Ray**: competitors, stores, products, URLs, ad libraries, Amazon listings, or brands.
- **Buyer Language X-Ray**: ICP/avatar/product category and a request for words, tone, objections, comments, reviews, pains, or desires.
- **Full Ecom Market X-Ray**: competitors plus ICP/product/category.
- **Offer/Angle X-Ray**: hooks, angles, mass desires, paid-social tests, PDP claims, offer stack, proof gaps, or creative strategy.

If the user did not specify output format, default to a chat brief. Use a spreadsheet for large competitor sets or language banks. Use a designed report when the user needs a client/shareable deliverable.

## Core Principles

- **Ecommerce buyers are not demographics first.** Start with desire, experience, behavior, and emotion; use demographics only when they explain buying behavior or media targeting.
- **Audience is not always buyer.** Separate the people watching content from the people buying the product.
- **Map awareness before message strategy.** Identify whether the market is unaware, problem aware, solution aware, product aware, or most aware before judging hooks, ads, PDPs, advertorials, or funnel gaps.
- **Validate mass desires before scaling angles.** Rank desires by urgency, staying power, scope, evidence density, and product fit.
- **Angles are belief shifts, not hooks, offers, formats, or templates.** A hook catches qualified attention; an offer changes the deal; an ad concept is the execution; the angle is the belief/frame the ad is built around.
- **Hooks should qualify, not merely catch.** Favor hooks that filter for the right buyer and emotional state over generic clickbait.
- **Competitor bad reviews are leverage.** One-star reviews reveal failed promises, product flaws, shipping/service wounds, trust gaps, and objections your offer or proof can answer.
- **Sophisticated ecommerce markets need more than bigger claims.** Look for new mechanisms, new information, new identity, or better proof that makes a familiar desire believable again.
- **Offer math matters.** Note AOV, bundles, quantity breaks, shipping thresholds, discounts, financing, subscriptions, warranties, guarantees, free gifts, post-purchase upsells, and margin-sensitive assumptions.
- **Iterate without breaking the winner.** When a desire, angle, hook, image, offer, or landing page is working, change one variable at a time.
- **Every claim gets a source and tier.** Do not blur confirmed facts, estimates, and inference.

## Evidence Tiers

Use these labels inline:

- **CONFIRMED**: seen on an owned or authoritative page, such as product page, checkout, official store, public ad library, Amazon listing, app listing, official social profile, or official policy page.
- **REPORTED**: self-reported or third-party estimate, such as traffic estimate, sales claim, follower count, revenue claim, review platform count, or marketplace estimate.
- **INFERRED**: reasoned from visible funnel structure, repeated copy, ads, reviews, or patterns.
- **RAW VOICE**: short verbatim customer language from a public post/comment/review with source.

Do not treat one Reddit comment, one review, or one YouTube reply as market truth. Treat it as a clue until repeated across sources.

## Workflow

### 1. Normalize Inputs

Classify every input:

- ecommerce brand/store/product name
- website, PDP, landing page, advertorial, Amazon/listing URL, TikTok Shop/Etsy/marketplace URL
- ad library, Shopify app/theme detector result, or social profile
- ICP/avatar/product category/problem statement
- user business context or offer anchor

For handles and brand names, chase obvious corrected spellings. If a target cannot be identified, say so and continue with the rest.

### 2. Load The Right References

For competitor/store/product work, read `references/research-agent.md`.

For buyer-language/ICP work, read `references/customer-language-mining.md`.

For final structure and deliverables, read `references/output-formats.md`.

For polished PDF/HTML reports, read `references/designed-report.md`.

### 3. Fan Out When Possible

If subagents are available and there is more than one competitor, product, or avatar segment, run one focused researcher per target in parallel. Require source URLs, offer details, review findings, and evidence tiers.

If subagents are not available, run the same process yourself one target at a time. Parallelism is for speed; evidence quality is the real standard.

### 4. Ecommerce Competitor Checklist

For each competitor, capture:

- identity: brand, product category, hero product, market, country, channel focus
- buyer/avatar: buyer vs audience, pains, desires, trigger moments, current alternatives
- funnel: ad -> landing page/PDP/advertorial -> cart -> checkout -> upsell -> subscription/retention
- offer stack: price, compare-at price, bundles, quantity breaks, BOGO, discount, gift, shipping threshold, financing, subscription, guarantee, warranty, returns, trial, urgency/scarcity
- proof: claims, mechanisms, ingredients/materials, studies, UGC, reviews, testimonials, before/after, authority, comparison tables
- creative strategy: awareness level, mass desire, angle, hook, ad concept, format, visual pattern, UGC/native/VSL/image/ad library notes
- landing-page/PDP strategy: above-the-fold promise, objections handled, FAQ, reviews, trust badges, CRO elements, page sequence
- Shopify tech stack: theme name/status, visible apps, app categories, detector source, where apps appear in the journey, confidence, and gaps for backend/private/obfuscated apps
- bad-review edge: product defects, service/shipping issues, unmet promises, trust gaps, proof gaps, competitor weaknesses
- quant snapshot: ad volume, review count/rating, Amazon rank if visible, social following, traffic range, store maturity signals, price range, visible bestsellers
- links directory: homepage, PDPs, landing pages, advertorials, ad library, socials, Amazon/marketplace listing, reviews, checkout/store links

### 5. Buyer-Language Mining Checklist

When the user gives an ICP, product category, or target buyer, keep searching until repeated language patterns are clear:

- Reddit threads and subreddit comments
- niche forums and public communities
- YouTube comments and replies on high-view niche videos
- TikTok comments when accessible
- Amazon reviews and Q&A, Trustpilot, Google reviews, Etsy, App Store, Meta shop, marketplace reviews
- competitor one-star and three-star reviews
- search autocomplete, People Also Ask, AnswerThePublic-style query surfaces
- podcasts/interviews/call transcripts when public or user-provided

Extract:

- exact phrases for pain, desire, failed attempts, trigger moments, fears, identity, objections, "I tried X", "I wish", "does anyone else", "finally", "worth it", and "didn't work"
- tone: skeptical, embarrassed, angry, clinical, practical, impatient, hopeful, status-driven, overwhelmed, relieved, etc.
- awareness level and sophistication: what claims they have heard, what they doubt, what proof they demand
- current behavior: products, routines, hacks, competitors, workarounds, or advice they already tried
- gap: why current behavior/product/competitor fails
- anti-language: phrases that feel fake, overclaimed, too clinical, cringe, or too marketer-ish

### 6. Synthesize For Ecommerce Execution

Always synthesize beyond raw notes:

- core buyer avatar and sub-avatar map
- mass desire shortlist with urgency, staying power, scope, evidence density, and product fit
- market awareness distribution and messaging implications
- belief/objection map, unpacked into experience, emotion, behavior, and desire
- competitor threat ranking using offer strength, proof strength, paid-social maturity, momentum, and customer-language fit
- language bank with reusable phrases and source links
- angle map: desire + behavior/current solution + gap -> belief shift -> hook direction -> proof needed
- offer gaps: what competitors promise, what buyers complain about, what the user can prove or bundle
- next tests: desire validation, angle tests, hook tests, native image/UGC tests, advertorial tests, PDP claim tests, offer/bundle tests, retention/upsell tests

## Quality Bar

Before delivering, check:

- At least one owned/authoritative source for each competitor when available.
- Price, offer, guarantee, shipping, Shopify theme, and visible app claims are sourced or explicitly marked not found.
- Customer-language clusters use multiple sources where possible.
- Quotes are short, linked, and used to reveal language patterns.
- The brief separates confirmed facts from reported estimates and inference.
- The final answer tells the user what was not verifiable, including Shopify apps or backend integrations that detectors cannot see.

If the user asks for a GitHub-ready artifact, include installation notes for both Codex and Claude outside the skill folder, not inside the skill itself.
